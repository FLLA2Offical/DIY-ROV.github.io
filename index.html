Welcome to Our DIY Underwater ROV Project!

We built a low-cost, functional underwater ROV for exploration and research. Designed for under $375, it features a wireless remote, a waterproof casing, and a camera for capturing underwater footage.

Key Features:

  • Affordable & Accessible – A budget-friendly alternative to commercial ROVs.
  • Custom Engineering – Efficient motor control, buoyancy adjustments, and durable waterproofing.
  • Easy to Operate – Wireless remote control for smooth navigation.
  • Built on Open-Source Platforms – Making adjustments easy.

Explore our design, wiring, and final product to see how we made underwater exploration possible!

Challenges

Building a functional ROV at an affordable price comes with several challenges:

  • Ensuring waterproofing and durability of all components.
  • Designing a stable and controllable navigation system.
  • Balancing the budget while maintaining high-quality parts.

Cost Breakdown

Traditional remotely operated vehicles (ROVs) cost between $3,000 and $140,000, limiting their accessibility to large organizations and research institutions. Our project focuses on designing and building a functional ROV for under $375, making it an ideal option for hobbyists, educators, and researchers on a budget.

The total cost of our ROV is kept under $375. Here's how the budget breaks down:

  • Motors: $100
  • Camera: $50
  • Buoyancy materials: $30
  • Controller and wiring: $80
  • Other materials (frame, sealing, etc.): $115

Design Process

We followed a step-by-step design approach:

  1. Initial concept and layout planning.
  2. Designing the frame for durability and lightweight properties.
  3. Testing for buoyancy and stability in water.
  4. Final adjustments and assembly.

We refined our design through multiple iterations, incorporating improvements in size, weight, and efficiency. Key changes included optimizing the frame structure for better hydrodynamics, upgrading propulsion for improved maneuverability, and refining waterproofing techniques to prevent water damage. An example from our design process includes the transition from a bulky external motor to compact brushless motors that allowed better speed and control. We also experimented with different buoyancy materials to achieve neutral buoyancy, making the ROV easier to control underwater.

Power and Energy Efficiency

The ROV is powered by a 12V battery, which is converted down to 5V for the Raspberry Pi and other electronics. To conserve energy, we use brushless motors known for higher efficiency and longevity. The system includes a power management module that regulates voltage and current, ensuring safe operation and preventing power surges.

Wiring and Electronics

The wiring layout includes connections from the battery to the power converter and then to the Raspberry Pi and motors. All wiring is waterproofed using sealant and carefully routed to avoid tangling or damage. We use a solderable breadboard to simplify the connections and allow easy modifications during testing.

Current Prototype

Our current prototype consists of a sealed waterproof case housing the Raspberry Pi, battery, and electronics. The brushless motors provide smooth propulsion, and the camera streams live video for navigation. The ROV can maneuver in all directions and maintain depth with the buoyancy system.

Code

The control software is written in Python, running on the Raspberry Pi. It handles input from the wireless remote, processes commands, and controls the motor speeds accordingly. The camera feed is streamed to a display device for real-time feedback.

# Sample motor control code (Python)

import RPi.GPIO as GPIO
import time

GPIO.setmode(GPIO.BCM)
motor_pins = [17, 18, 27, 22]  # Example motor control pins
for pin in motor_pins:
    GPIO.setup(pin, GPIO.OUT)
    GPIO.output(pin, GPIO.LOW)

def forward():
    GPIO.output(17, GPIO.HIGH)
    GPIO.output(18, GPIO.LOW)
    GPIO.output(27, GPIO.HIGH)
    GPIO.output(22, GPIO.LOW)

def stop():
    for pin in motor_pins:
        GPIO.output(pin, GPIO.LOW)

try:
    while True:
        forward()
        time.sleep(2)
        stop()
        time.sleep(2)
except KeyboardInterrupt:
    GPIO.cleanup()
